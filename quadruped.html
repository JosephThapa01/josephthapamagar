üìã About This Project

The 3D-printed 8-DOF quadruped robot is a low-cost robotics platform designed to explore mechanical design, embedded systems, and gait control. The project demonstrates the design, prototyping, and iterative optimization process required to build a walking quadruped using affordable components, while achieving smooth and stable locomotion.

Mechanical Design

Chassis and Frame

  • The legs which mainly includes tibia and femur, are fully 3D-printed, providing a lightweight and rigid structure.
  • The leg was designed to minimize flex, maintain low center of gravity, and support dynamic movements without overloading the joints.
  • The robot‚Äôs frame was built from MDF board, providing a sturdy base to attach the legs and other components.

Leg Joints and Degrees of Freedom

Each leg contains two servo-actuated joints:

  • Hip joint ‚Äì provides forward-backward swing.
  • Knee joint ‚Äì provides vertical lift for foot clearance.
  • Total robot degrees of freedom: 8 (2 per leg).

Early prototypes suffered from high inertia and unstable leg motion, as servos mounted at the extremities increased weight and torque demand. Repositioning servos closer to the body reduced rotational inertia, improving response time and dynamic stability.

4-Bar Linkage Mechanism

"A planar 4-bar linkage is used in each leg to control the tibia (knee) joint, enabling the servo motors to be positioned on the robot‚Äôs body rather than directly on the legs, reducing leg weight and improving stability.

Benefits:

  • Stable Center of Gravity: Servo placement on the body ensures the center of gravity remains constant during leg movement, preventing tipping.
  • Reduced Leg Weight: Legs are lighter since servos are not mounted directly on them, improving efficiency and speed.
  • Natural Motion: Enables leg movement patterns that closely mimic real quadrupeds.

Gait and Motion Control

Trot Gait

The robot employs a trot gait, where diagonal leg pairs move together:

  • Front-left & back-right, then front-right & back-left.

Advantages:

  • Provides dynamic stability even with lightweight frames.
  • Requires fewer timing adjustments than a fully independent leg gait.

Control Architecture

  • Gait is controlled by Python scripts running on the Raspberry Pi.
  • PWM outputs are sent through the PCA9685 driver to ensure smooth, synchronized servo motion.
  • The control loop computes joint angles for each servo based on gait phase and desired stride length.

Motion Features

  • Center of gravity management: The 4-bar linkage and servo placement maintain CoG stability.
  • Remote control: Wi-Fi Flask server allows real-time adjustment of gait parameters (step height, speed, direction).
  • Smooth trajectories: The combination of Python interpolation and mechanical linkage prevents abrupt foot placement, reducing tipping risk.

Challenges and Iterative Improvements

Early Prototypes

  • Flex in 3D-printed legs led to wobbling.
  • Heavy legs with distal-mounted servos caused torque overload and instability.

Servo Placement Optimization

  • Moving servos closer to the body reduced leg inertia, improving acceleration and reducing tipping.

Center of Gravity and Weight Distribution

  • Careful positioning of the battery, servos, and electronics ensured stability during dynamic gait cycles.

Linkage Geometry

  • Fine-tuning the 4-bar linkage improved foot trajectory accuracy and repeatability.
  • Ball bearings reduced friction and extended servo lifespan.

Gait Tuning

  • Step length, speed, and timing were adjusted iteratively to achieve consistent trot without loss of balance.

Learning Outcomes

  • Mechanical Design: Practical application of legged robot kinematics, 4-bar linkage mechanics, and low-weight structural design.
  • Embedded Systems: PWM control of multiple servos via PCA9685, real-time servo synchronization, and Python-based control loops.
  • Gait & Motion Control: Implementation of trot gait, trajectory planning, and center of gravity management.
  • Iterative Engineering: Problem-solving through repeated testing, prototyping, and component repositioning.
  • Remote Robotics: Integration of Wi-Fi control for experimentation with gait parameters.
